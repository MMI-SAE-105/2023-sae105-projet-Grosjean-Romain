The Science of Jurassic Park

On a scientific level, how are extinct life forms brought back to life?

The Science of Jurassic Park and dinosaur media

Written by GROSJEAN Romain

Published on 18 October 2023

Reading time: 2'min

Jurassic Park is a film that sparks scientific curiosity and imagination. It offers a fascinating glimpse into the resurrection of long-extinct species through the wonders of genetic engineering. But how close does the science of the film come to reality, and what does it really take to bring back to life species that disappeared from the face of the Earth thousands or even millions of years ago?

In the book The Science of Jurassic Park and The Lost World: How to Make a Dinosaur, American authors and researchers David Lindley and Rob DeSalle explore the scientific aspects of the film and the wider world of dinosaur recreation. They address essential questions about the possibility of resurrecting extinct species, and ultimately explore the real science behind the fiction.

As far as Jurassic Park is concerned, it's true that the film presents a captivating portrait, albeit a fictional one, and with hindsight biased in many respects, of the cloning of dinosaurs from DNA preserved in the blood collected from mosquitoes and eventually trapped in amber. In reality, this process is far more complex and it is impossible to recover dinosaur DNA or even anything else in this way. We have a better chance of recovering the DNA of the mosquito itself in this way, and even then!

The book raises profound questions about the ethics and implications of de-extinction, and plunges us into the role of science, human ambition and commercial interests in the pursuit of “resurrected” species. Is it worth it to reviving the dodo or the mammoths? The book serves as a bridge between cinematic spectacle and the fascinating world of science, offering us readers a better understanding of the film's themes and the ongoing efforts to unravel the mysteries of our prehistoric past.
